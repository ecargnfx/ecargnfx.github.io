peeq.it

How do we learn about our world if it's not textually searchable? As a visual person, I'm constantly curious about my surroundings, but there's no way to learn more about what I'm looking at if I do not know the vocabulary to input into a text search.

In 2010, I created my first startup; a photo Q&A mobile app that aimed to improve search and image recognition. The design started with a normal list interface, but I wanted the experience to be more immersive. This redesign conveys the concept "explore the world from your backyard" and leveraged new gestures like pinch and tap hold to close or reveal more content. But when we launched, we couldn't get engaged users. I was frustrated and determined to find out why. When I conducted customer development, I realized this solution didn't solve for a big enough problem.

Crayola

Problem: I redesigned an app for Crayola. The client wanted to use the accelerometer to draw in the air, but the original interface (far right) had buttons that prevented the user from seeing what they were drawing.

Solution: I tucked away unnecessary UI in favor of gestural controls. In the final version, we enlarged and spaced out the crayons to allow easier tapping. Using the iPhone's accelerometer and gyroscope, the app not only lets you "draw in space" but also animates your doodle in preview mode with a simple tilt of your phone. Talk about breathing life into art.
